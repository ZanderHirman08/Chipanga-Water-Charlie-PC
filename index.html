The project

Over the past two years, living in Chipanga, Charlie has worked with the local community to solve a basic problem: reliable water for a school and for the families who farm nearby. That work is now becoming physical infrastructure — a borehole, a solar-powered pump, water storage tanks, and two garden sites that will support a school garden and a ten-plot women's community garden on roughly one acre of land.

This page exists to show investors and funders exactly what has been built, what's planned next, and the water and food-production numbers behind it — using survey data collected on the ground with QField/QGIS.

Funding so far

Grant from the Longmont Rotary Club, funding the borehole itself — a 6-foot-wide, 180-meter-deep well.

Global Rotary network

Additional support from community Rotary clubs around the world, coordinated through Rotary International's international project funding.

Infrastructure

Borehole

6 ft wide, 180 m deep. Maximum sustainable output: 5,000 liters/hour.

Solar pump system

Submersible pump at the bottom of the well, wired to a solar array at the surface, next to the borehole. The array and pump controller sit inside a surveyed chain-link fence.

Tanks (2 mapped)

A tank for the school garden and a tank for the women's garden, each recorded on site at 8,000 gallons (~30,000 L). A separate drinking-water tank for the school building itself hasn't been sited yet.

Piping network

Four runs mapped so far: the bore hole pipe (well to garden area), the school head pipe (toward the school), the main women's garden pipe, and a sub pipe running the length of a garden bed. Five taps are in on the women's garden line.

Garden plots

Women's garden: ~0.75 acre, complete and tilled — 10 individual plots to be divided within it. School garden: one 15 × 45 m bed built so far; two more identical beds are planned directly behind it, bringing the finished plot to 45 × 45 m (~0.5 acre) once complete.

School

Adjacent to the garden site; the piping network's other endpoint.
