The project

The project started Charlie's first week in the village, at the end of the dry season — the hottest time of year, almost no greenery aside from scattered trees. He was introduced to a local farmer, Mama Sophia, and her farm along the riverbed, where she and five other women had set up vegetable gardens fed by a seasonal well they'd dug by hand — fetching water by bucket, morning and evening. The following dry season Charlie worked with the group to pool resources for a gas-powered pump. It helped, but limited water access and conflicts with the landowner over water allocation made clear that the group needed a real solution.

Alongside that, Charlie spent his first year at the village's primary school — visiting often, sitting in on classes, learning about its nutritional and resource gaps. Without a reliable water source, students relied on rainwater and the same dry-season wells for basic drinking and hygiene needs. There was no food available at school, so students either walked home for lunch — meaning many from farther away simply didn't return for the afternoon — or stayed the whole day with nothing to eat. An attempt at a school garden ran into the same water restrictions as the women's group.

That's where the project took shape: two groups with the same goals and resource needs, able to mutually support each other. The school could provide land access; the women's group brought labor, knowledge, and financial support. Together, a reliable water source would meet the school's basic water needs and let both groups grow a meaningful supply of vegetables. About a year after Charlie started pricing out a borehole and pump system, the village has a working borehole and solar pump, and is now building out almost two acres of vegetable gardens and a water storage and distribution network.

Infrastructure

Borehole

6 ft wide, 180 m deep. Maximum sustainable output: 5,000 liters/hour.

Solar pump system

A DIFFUL heavy-duty solar submersible pump, powered by 12 high-capacity 580W solar panels (~7 kW array), wired to the pump at the bottom of the well. The array and pump controller sit inside a surveyed chain-link fence next to the borehole.

Tanks & water pressure

Two 8,000 L tanks (2 m wide, 2.5 m tall) — one for the women's garden, one for the school garden — plus a separate 4,000 L tank (1.6 m wide, 2 m tall) for the primary school building. Each garden tank sits on a 3 m stand, gravity-feeding the lines at 0.3–0.55 bar depending on fill level. Tanks are refilled morning and afternoon to stay above half-full and keep pressure up.

Piping network

1.5" HDPE pipe — Class D (thicker, higher-pressure) from the borehole, Class C for distribution. Runs are about 90 m to the far end of the women's garden line and 80 m on the school side. The gravity system supports 4 taps open at once without a significant pressure drop.

Watering approach

Starting with hand watering from hoses and buckets rather than drip irrigation — a deliberate choice. Drip lines need regular maintenance and replacement, and the water's salt content clogs emitters quickly; in a rural setting without reliable parts or repair access, that risk outweighs the water savings. Instead, a local community member is paid a monthly wage for daily watering and garden upkeep — putting the investment into steady local income instead of imported hardware.

Garden plots

Women's garden: ~0.75 acre, complete and tilled — 10 individual plots to be divided within it. School garden: one 15 × 45 m bed built so far; two more identical beds are planned directly behind it, bringing the finished plot to 45 × 45 m (~0.5 acre) once complete. Together, almost two acres of vegetable gardens.

Long-term sustainability & governance

The water system is run by a project committee made up of teachers, school board members, women's group members, and two other influential community members. They meet every month to review challenges, collect water fees billed monthly to the school and the women's group, and allocate those funds — covering a security guard and system maintenance. As monthly contributions build up in a shared bank account, the project accumulates enough to cover recurring costs and build real savings, so it stays self-sufficient long after outside funding stops.

Support the next phase

The borehole and solar pump are in and running. What's left — tank stands (finishing early August 2026), the irrigation lines (mid-August), and getting the school lunch program off the ground — is what turns a working well into two fully productive gardens and a daily meal for 630 students.

Feeding students from the school garden

Once the school garden is fully producing, the harvest can supply free vegetables for regular student lunches. 630 students would eat 5 days a week. The remaining cost — corn, beans, cooking oil, salt, cooking labor, and fuel, plus a 25% buffer for late payments and price swings — comes to a monthly contribution of 10,000 Tanzanian shillings. The school is holding its first parent meeting on this soon; the expected pushback is the cost itself, and low trust that meals will really be provided every day or that enough parents will actually pay in. That's exactly the kind of gap outside support can help close while local buy-in builds.
